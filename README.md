## I. The Contingent Esteem Architecture

Crocker and Park's (2004) analysis of contingent self-esteem established the foundational mechanism: when a person's self-esteem is organized around outcomes in a specific domain, events in that domain carry weight that extends far beyond their informational content. A loss in the domain does not merely disappoint; it diminishes. A competitor's victory in the domain does not merely register; it injures. The contingent esteem architecture converts comparative information into self-worth signal at a ratio that Crocker and Park document as having direct consequences for motivation, relationship quality, and persistent life outcomes.

For the older sibling and peer in the ERIE-SCHOOL lineage's primary competitive contexts — the family system that valorizes cognitive achievement; the academic environment where intellectual performance is the central evaluative dimension — intelligence and academic performance are maximally self-esteem-contingent. The child and adolescent who has built their identity around their intellectual standing in the family and peer system has organized their self-worth around the domain the outlier will dominate. Leary and Baumeister's (2000) sociometer theory specifies the social function of this contingency: self-esteem is a sociometer — an internal measure of current social acceptance and standing — and its fluctuations track fluctuations in perceived acceptance and status. The suppressor whose esteem is organized around intellectual standing is not merely experiencing subjective pain when the outlier outperforms them. They are receiving a sociometer reading that their social position has deteriorated.

Baumeister, Smart, and Boden's (1996) foundational analysis of threatened egotism establishes what follows from this deterioration signal. High self-esteem combined with ego threat — not low self-esteem, not threat to a person with secure self-regard, but specifically the combination of prior high self-esteem with a challenge to it — is the profile most reliably associated with hostile, aggressive, and destructive responding. The dangerous suppressor is not someone with fragile self-esteem who feels generally worthless. They are someone who has occupied a position of relative intellectual superiority in their prior competitive context — who has been the smart one in the family, the strong student in the classroom, the recognized intellectual contributor in the peer group — and who now encounters, in the outlier, a challenge to that position that their prior self-concept cannot absorb.

The mechanism is precise: the ego threat makes the threatened dimension available as the source of hostility, and the hostility is directed specifically at the person who constitutes the threat. The suppressor who has organized their self-esteem around intellectual standing and who encounters the outlier does not experience diffuse or generalized hostility. They experience specific hostility toward the outlier — the person whose very existence constitutes the threat — motivated by the same self-protective dynamics that produce any defensive aggression in response to perceived self-concept damage.

Zeigler-Hill's (2006) analysis of discrepancies between explicit and implicit self-esteem adds a critical dimension. The suppressor who presents high explicit self-esteem — who reports, and perhaps genuinely believes, that they feel confident about themselves — may carry significant implicit self-esteem insecurity at the level that automatic social processing reveals. The discrepancy between high explicit and lower implicit self-esteem predicts narcissistic responding, defensive hostility to ego threats, and the specific behavioral signature that Morf and Rhodewalt (2001) document in their dynamic self-regulatory processing model of narcissism: the need for chronic external validation of the positive self-view, combined with aggressive responses to any social information that challenges it.

The **Contingent Esteem Architecture** is therefore not merely a background condition for the suppressor's behavior. It is the generating mechanism. The suppressor's esteem is contingent on their relative intellectual standing; the outlier's superiority directly diminishes that standing through the sociometer's operation; the diminishment activates the threatened-egotism response profile that Baumeister and colleagues identify; and the response directs specific hostility toward the outlier as the source of the threat. At no point in this sequence does the suppressor need to consciously intend harm, plan a campaign, or recognize themselves as engaging in suppressive behavior. The sequence runs automatically, below the threshold of deliberate choice, driven by the same motivational architecture that produces any self-protective response to any threat to any high-stakes self-concept.

The CEA's most consequential property is its immunity to ordinary social correction. The suppressor whose behavior is challenged — told that their characterization of the outlier is unfair — cannot process this correction through normal prosocial channels because the correction itself constitutes a further threat to the self-concept the behavior is protecting. Accepting the correction would require simultaneously accepting the outlier's superiority, which is precisely what the CEA cannot accommodate without self-worth damage. Correction does not weaken the CEA. It activates it further.

## II. The Threatened Incumbent: Positional Psychology of the Older Sibling and Senior Peer

The older sibling's developmental position is structurally prior to the outlier's arrival: they occupied the first-child position in the family system before the outlier existed, and with it, the first-child's automatic claim to parental attention, relational primacy, and the cognitive and social resources that first-occupant position confers. Sulloway's (1996) evolutionary analysis of birth order and family dynamics establishes this structural primacy as a real and consequential developmental fact: firstborn children show consistent personality profiles — conscientiousness, achievement motivation, resistance to change — that reflect the motivational dynamics of the first-occupant position in a resource-competitive family ecology.

The outlier's developmental emergence disrupts this positional stability in a specific and asymmetric way. The older sibling who was the recognized intellectual achiever in the family — who held the position of the smart, capable, impressive child — encounters, in the outlier's development, an unbidden and uncontrollable revision to their positional standing. Feinberg, McHale, Crouter, and Cumsille's (2003) developmental research on sibling differentiation establishes that parental comparison between siblings is among the most reliably toxic processes in sibling relationship development: when parents explicitly or implicitly compare siblings' performances, the sibling who is unfavorably compared experiences the comparison as a direct positional demotion and responds through the differentiation and competitive processes the prior corpus documents. McHale, Updegraff, Jackson-Newsom, Tucker, and Crouter's (2000) research confirms that differential parental treatment predicts sibling relationship quality deterioration, with unfavorably treated siblings showing elevated hostility, relational aggression, and competitive interference.

The older sibling's position has a specific vulnerability that the younger sibling's position does not: the threat of being surpassed by someone who was once unambiguously junior. In competitive contexts where status is organized around performance rather than birth position, a younger sibling who surpasses an older sibling's performance level represents a positional violation — the reversal of a developmental ordering that the older sibling's entire social identity has been structured around. This is the threatened-incumbent dynamic in its most complete form: not merely competition between equals but the specific threat of being surpassed by one's prior dependent.

Emerson's (1962) power-dependence analysis specifies why this specific threat profile generates the most extreme suppressant response. When the formerly subordinate party demonstrates that they will eventually dominate the competitive domain, the formerly dominant party's entire positional structure is at risk. The suppressant response is not merely competitive — it is the response of an incumbent protecting a position they have occupied and organized their identity around since before the challenger existed.

The specific older peer's threatened-incumbent psychology operates through the same mechanisms as the sibling's, with birth position replaced by the seniority and prior-claim that senior-peer status confers. The peer who is two or three years ahead — who has established credentials and authority-figure relationships in a shared domain, who has been the recognized capable one in the relevant social context — faces in the developmental outlier the same structural challenge: a junior party who threatens to surpass the domain standing the senior party's entire local identity is organized around. The seniority premium that should confer permanent positional advantage is revealed, by the outlier's developmental trajectory, as a temporal accident rather than a permanent structural fact.

Schachter, Shore, Feldman-Rotman, Marquis, and Campbell's (1976) foundational sibling deidentification research documents the defensive response: siblings and peers who cannot compete viably in the outlier's strongest domain undergo motivated deidentification from that domain, claiming differentiated identities that minimize direct comparison. What the prior document (COMPETITIVE-EVACUATION) established about the peer who exits is the non-threatened-incumbent response. The suppressor is the sibling or peer for whom exit from the domain is not psychologically available — whose domain investment is too deep, whose positional identity too consolidated, whose prior standing too public and too important to the self-concept to be abandoned without complete identity dissolution. The suppressor is the one who cannot leave because leaving would cost more than staying and fighting. The fight is the only option remaining that is consistent with the self-concept they have built.

## III. The Motivated Attribution Engine

Kunda's (1990) foundational analysis of motivated reasoning established the mechanism through which people construct, rather than merely reach, conclusions that serve their motivational ends. The motivated reasoner does not simply endorse whatever feels comfortable; they engage in genuine cognitive work — searching for evidence, constructing arguments, evaluating logical connections — with the work governed by a prior directional constraint: find reasons for the preferred conclusion. The result is reasoning that appears to the reasoner as rigorous and independent but is, at the structural level, the product of a goal the reasoning process never made explicit to itself.

The **Motivated Attribution Engine** is the specific application of Kunda's motivated reasoning to the suppressor's social perception of the outlier. The suppressor's CEA requires the outlier to be inferior, ordinary, and arrogant. This requirement does not typically operate as a conscious perceptual instruction — the suppressor does not deliberately decide to see arrogance where there is none. Instead, the MAE converts every ambiguous social signal from the outlier into evidence for the CEA's required conclusion through five simultaneous attribution mechanisms that each operate below deliberate choice.

The first mechanism is **upward comparison threat management**: the attribution of the outlier's successes to luck, circumstance, or the absence of genuine competition. Mezulis, Abramson, Hyde, and Hankin's (2004) meta-analytic review of attributional biases confirms that attributing others' successes to external factors is among the most universal and robustly documented motivated-cognition patterns, appearing across cultures, domains, and age groups. For the suppressor, this attribution is specifically amplified by the CEA's esteem-protecting function: if the outlier's success is external, the outlier's superiority is circumstantial, and the CEA's self-worth-protecting function is preserved. The attribution is not chosen. It is produced by a perceptual system whose function is to protect the suppressor's self-regard from damage the outlier's objective superiority would otherwise cause.

The second mechanism is **downward comparison satisfaction**: the attribution of the outlier's failures and difficulties to underlying incapacity. While the suppressor systematically externalizes the outlier's successes, they systematically internalize the outlier's failures — reading each failure as diagnostic of the underlying limitations they have always suspected. Feather and Sherman's (2002) schadenfreude research establishes that failures by resented high achievers produce more intense schadenfreude than failures by liked peers, and that the pleasure is specifically tied to the inference that the failure reveals the original achievement as inflated. For the suppressor, the outlier's occasional failure is not merely satisfying — it is the confirmation event the MAE has been calibrated to detect and process as validation.

The third mechanism is **hostile attribution bias**: the systematic interpretation of the outlier's ambiguous behaviors as reflecting hostile or arrogant intent. Dodge's (1986) social information processing model established that children with aggressive behavioral profiles show a consistent bias toward hostile attribution — interpreting ambiguous social cues as indicating that others intend harm. The suppressor applies this bias specifically to the outlier: the outlier's intellectual confidence is read as arrogance rather than as self-assessment; their direct communication style is read as dismissiveness rather than efficiency; their persistence in intellectual argument is read as aggression rather than conviction. Each reading is plausible on its surface. The bias is what makes it systematic.

The fourth mechanism is **self-presentational attribution inversion**: the attribution of the suppressor's own suppressant behavior to legitimate and beneficial motives. Schlenker's (2003) self-presentation research establishes that people engage in motivated attribution of their own behavior — constructing post-hoc narratives that explain their behavior in terms of admirable rather than self-serving motives. The suppressor who has just executed a Level III reputation contamination campaign against the outlier does not, in their self-concept, experience themselves as having executed a campaign. They experience themselves as having shared honest concern about a person whose behavior genuinely troubles them, with people who deserve to know what interacting with the outlier is like.

The fifth mechanism is **projection of competitive motivation**: the attribution of the suppressor's own competitive aggression to the outlier. Newman, Duff, and Baumeister's (1997) defensive projection research established that people who are suppressing awareness of their own socially undesirable traits show elevated projection of those traits onto others. The suppressor who cannot acknowledge their own competitive aggression — which, if acknowledged, would violate the Justified-Perceiver self-concept — projects that aggression onto the outlier, who is then perceived as the one behaving competitively, aggressively, or self-promotingly. The projection resolves the suppressor's cognitive dissonance: the competitive aggression the situation requires is present, but it belongs to the outlier, not to the suppressor.

These five mechanisms operate simultaneously, reinforcing each other and the CEA's required conclusion. The MAE is not a single cognitive bias. It is a system of mutually reinforcing misattributions whose combined output is a stable, self-confirming perception of the outlier as arrogant, overrated, and difficult — a perception the suppressor experiences as accurate, has constructed through what feels like careful observation, and can defend with what appear to be specific examples. The examples are real. The perception is mechanism-generated. Ditto and Lopez's (1992) analysis of motivated skepticism specifies the asymmetric evidential processing that makes the MAE self-sustaining: evidence confirming the caricature is accepted at a low threshold; evidence disconfirming the caricature is subjected to intensive skeptical processing before being either discounted or reabsorbed through one of the five attribution mechanisms.

## IV. The Moral Disengagement Architecture

Bandura's (1999) moral disengagement model identified the cognitive operations through which people who consider themselves moral actors nevertheless engage in behavior that, accurately labeled, would violate their moral self-concept. The model specifies eight distinct disengagement operations that convert the moral self-regulation system's normal functioning into a form that does not fire on harmful behavior because the behavior has been cognitively reconstructed into something other than harm.

The **Moral Disengagement Architecture** as operated by the suppressor is a specific instantiation of Bandura's model, adapted to the social competitive context and organized around four primary disengagement operations that are particularly available given the suppressor's social position, relationship history, and the cultural frameworks available in the competitive developmental environment.

The first is **moral justification**: the construction of a narrative in which the suppression serves a moral goal that transcends its harm. The suppressor who characterizes the outlier as arrogant to a new acquaintance is not, in their self-concept, executing a reputation contamination campaign. They are performing the socially necessary function of warning someone who has no prior experience with the outlier about what they will encounter — providing accurate information to protect a person from being misled by the outlier's surface performance. The justification is available precisely because the outlier's genuine capacity does make them unusual, and unusual people sometimes genuinely are arrogant. The moral justification recruits this genuine possibility and installs it as the default interpretation, regardless of the actual evidence.

The second is **displacement of responsibility**: the attribution of the suppressor's behavior to external conditions that left them no choice — specifically, to the outlier's behavior as the provocation that necessitated the suppressor's response. When harmful behavior can be attributed to another's prior provocation, the agent of harm constructs themselves as responding rather than initiating. The suppressor's political bypass against the outlier is not initiated by the suppressor. It is a response to the outlier's arrogance, or their competitive behavior, or their failure to manage their impact on others. The displaced responsibility converts the suppressor from operator to reactor — from someone executing a campaign to someone simply responding, as anyone would, to genuinely difficult behavior.

The third is **dehumanization through caricature**: the cognitive reduction of the outlier to their caricature attributes, which strips them of the full personhood that makes harm to them feel morally prohibited. Bandura's (1999) research on dehumanization in institutional violence shows that moral inhibitions against harming others depend on perceiving the other as a full person with subjective experience. The caricature — arrogant, difficult, uncollaborative — is a reduction of the outlier from a complex person to a type, and types do not carry the full moral standing that persons do. The suppressor who has fully installed the caricature no longer perceives the outlier as a full subject; they perceive a character — the Arrogant One, the Difficult One, the Person Who Never Acknowledges Others — and characters do not trigger the moral inhibitions against harm that persons do.

The fourth is **advantageous comparison**: the evaluation of the suppressor's behavior not against an abstract moral standard but against a comparison that makes their behavior appear moderated and reasonable. The suppressor who has executed an extensive reputation contamination campaign against the outlier did not, after all, physically harm them, deny them access to resources through formal means, or engage in behavior that anyone would legally identify as wrong. Compared to that standard, the suppressor's behavior — honest expression of concern to trusted people — appears not merely acceptable but actively virtuous.

The MDA produces a specific experiential quality in the suppressor that is critical for understanding the suppression architecture's durability: the suppressor feels morally comfortable. They are not suppressing awareness of having done something wrong. They have engaged in a cognitive reconstruction through the four operations above that converts the suppression into moral enterprise from which the suppressor derives positive moral self-regard — the satisfaction of having provided honest service and accurate perception rather than the discomfort of having harmed someone. Bandura's (2002) selective moral disengagement research specifies that this disengagement is domain-specific rather than globally disabling: the suppressor whose MDA is active in their behavior toward the outlier remains functionally moral in other domains. Their general prosocial behavior — in other relationships, in public contexts, in community roles — is genuine, which is precisely what makes their self-concept as an honest person credible to themselves and to the social environment. The suppressor is not a bad person. They are a good person who has activated domain-specific moral disengagement in the one competitive context where their self-concept requires it.

## V. The Negative Identity Anchor

Tajfel and Turner's (1986) social identity theory established that group membership is constitutive of self-concept: people derive positive self-regard not only from personal achievement but from membership in groups that compare favorably to relevant out-groups. The key dynamic is positive distinctiveness — the need for one's group to be positively differentiated from comparison groups on valued dimensions. When positive distinctiveness is threatened — when the in-group's valued characteristics are challenged by a comparison — social identity theory predicts the full range of protecting responses: in-group enhancement, out-group derogation, and intergroup competition.

The **Negative Identity Anchor** is the application of this dynamic at the level of the individual self-concept rather than the group. The suppressor's positive identity characteristics — warmth, collaborative spirit, intellectual humility, social attunement — are not merely personal qualities. They are defined in contrast to their opposite, and the opposite is the outlier as characterized by the caricature. The suppressor is warm because the outlier is cold. The suppressor is collaborative because the outlier is arrogant. The suppressor is intellectually humble because the outlier is intellectually overweening. The caricature is not just a characterization of the outlier. It is the foundation of the suppressor's own positive self-definition.

This is the NIA's constitutive property, and it is what distinguishes it from ordinary social comparison. In ordinary social comparison, the self-concept is enhanced by comparing favorably to others on specific dimensions. In the NIA structure, the self-concept is defined by contrast to a specific person — the outlier — whose negative characteristics are the mirror image of the suppressor's positive ones. The suppressor does not merely compare favorably to the outlier; they require the outlier to have the negative characteristics the caricature specifies in order to maintain the positive characteristics their self-concept claims. If the outlier is not arrogant, the suppressor's humility is not defined against anything. If the outlier is not cold, the suppressor's warmth is not set off against a foil. The caricature's maintenance is not merely competitive. It is constitutive.

Sedikides and Strube's (1997) analysis of self-evaluation motivation specifies four concurrent motivations that govern self-concept maintenance: self-assessment, self-enhancement, self-improvement, and self-verification. The NIA operates primarily through self-enhancement and self-verification simultaneously: the caricature enhances the suppressor's self-concept by providing a negative foil against which positive qualities are defined, and it verifies the suppressor's self-concept by ensuring that the social reality the suppressor encounters confirms the characteristics they need to believe they have.

The NIA predicts a specific behavioral consequence that has not been explicitly identified in the prior analyses: the suppressor will respond with particular intensity to disconfirmation of the caricature. The caricature is not merely a hypothesis about the outlier that can be revised in the face of disconfirming evidence. It is a constitutive element of the suppressor's positive self-concept, which means that disconfirming evidence for the caricature is simultaneously threatening evidence for the suppressor's own positive self-definition. When someone challenges the caricature — reporting that they found the outlier warm and thoughtful — the suppressor does not receive this as information that they may have been wrong about the outlier. They receive it as information that threatens the positive qualities they have defined in contrast to the caricature. The result is not revision but intensification: more elaborate defense of the caricature, more confident assertion of its accuracy, more active recruitment of confirming evidence. The NIA makes disconfirmation threatening rather than informative, which is why the caricature is structurally resistant to the accumulation of contrary behavioral evidence that should, in any ordinary hypothesis-testing framework, eventually force revision.

Swann's (1990) self-verification research specified exactly this dynamic at the level of interaction behavior: people whose self-concepts are challenged do not accept the challenge and revise. They seek out and create interaction conditions that confirm the self-concept under challenge. For the suppressor, the self-concept under challenge whenever the outlier demonstrates warmth or humility is precisely the caricature's set of negative attributes — which means every disconfirming interaction activates in the suppressor the self-verification motivation to restore the negative characterization. The restoration is executed through the mechanisms the prior corpus documents: increased caricature maintenance activity, more confident characterization to authority figures, more active coalition support for the caricature's accuracy. Disconfirmation accelerates the suppression rather than terminating it.

## VI. The Social Reward Matrix

The suppression architecture is not sustained by internal motivation alone. It receives external reinforcement through a structured social reward system whose operation the prior analyses have documented from the outside — the coalition formation, the authority figure cultivation, the Credibility Inversion's feedback — but whose function as an operant conditioning apparatus for the suppressor's own behavior has not been specified.

Bandura's (1973) social learning analysis of aggressive behavior established the foundational principle: behavior that is rewarded increases in frequency and intensity, regardless of whether the behavior is prosocial or harmful. The suppressor's behavior is specifically and multiply rewarded by the social environment in which it operates, through four simultaneous reinforcement channels.

The first channel is **coalition social reinforcement**. The Level II coalition that the suppressor assembles around the caricature — peer group members who have accepted and propagated the characterization — provides continuous social reward for caricature maintenance. Each time a coalition member confirms the characterization, expresses solidarity with the suppressor's position, or demonstrates that the caricature has been received and endorsed, the suppressor receives the social belonging and status reward that acceptance provides. The coalition does not merely agree with the suppressor — they position the suppressor as the socially central, accurately-perceiving figure around whom their shared social reality is organized. The status reward from coalition leadership is powerful and sustained.

The second channel is **authority figure validation**. The Credibility Inversion the prior corpus documents produces a specific reward for the suppressor: the authority figure who has been cultivated and contaminated reflects back to the suppressor the characterization they deposited. When the authority figure expresses concern about the outlier's arrogance, or reserves their warmest endorsement for the suppressor rather than the outlier, the suppressor receives two simultaneous rewards: confirmation that the caricature is accurate (the trusted authority figure independently reached the same conclusion) and direct validation of the suppressor's own social standing (the authority has chosen them over the outlier). The authority figure's validation converts the suppressor's subjectively motivated perception into what appears, to the suppressor and to the social environment, as objective institutional assessment.

The third channel is **the outlier's mechanism-generated behavioral confirmation**. The prior corpus's Behavioral Confirmation Engine specifies how the suppressor's altered interaction pattern elicits from the outlier the social misfires that confirm the caricature. These mechanism-generated behavioral confirmations are experienced by the suppressor as independent validation of their perception — not as evidence of what their behavior caused but as evidence of what they correctly predicted. The outlier who shows social awkwardness in the suppressor's presence (produced by the BCE's interaction-quality degradation) is perceived by the suppressor as confirmation that the awkwardness characterization was accurate. Every confirmation strengthens the suppressor's conviction in the caricature and their commitment to the suppression architecture.

The fourth channel is **the schadenfreude reward from the outlier's difficulties**. Cikara and Fiske's (2012) research on schadenfreude established that pleasure at a rival's misfortune is physiologically measurable and particularly intense when the rival had previously outperformed the observer on a socially relevant dimension. For the suppressor whose CEA has been organized around the outlier's intellectual dominance, the outlier's failures, setbacks, and difficulties provide specific hedonic reward through the schadenfreude mechanism — reward that reinforces the suppressor's investment in monitoring for and facilitating those difficulties. The suppression architecture's tendency to channel the outlier toward developmental hazard is partly a motivated pursuit of this reward: engineering conditions for the outlier's difficulty produces the specific schadenfreude that the contingent esteem architecture is calibrated to receive.

The **Social Reward Matrix's** most important property is that it converts what begins as a motivated response to threat into a stable behavioral habit. The initial suppressant behaviors were produced by the CEA's threatened-egotism mechanism. But through the four reward channels, those behaviors are operantly conditioned — rewarded consistently and multiply — into a stable behavioral repertoire that continues regardless of whether the initial threat remains acute. The suppressor who has been rewarded for caricature maintenance across years of coalition confirmation and authority figure validation does not require the outlier's current active threat to the CEA to continue the behavior. The behavior has been conditioned into a stable pattern that runs on its own reinforcement history. This is why the suppression architecture is typically most intense and elaborated by adulthood — at the stage when the outlier's performance advantage should be most obvious — rather than declining as the evidence accumulates: the SRM's conditioning has consolidated a behavior pattern that the CEA's initial mechanism no longer needs to actively drive.

## VII. The Projective Apparatus

Newman, Duff, and Baumeister's (1997) defensive projection research showed that when people suppress awareness of their own undesirable traits — traits they hold but cannot acknowledge without self-concept damage — those traits become hyperaccessible in social perception, leading to elevated projection of the traits onto others. The suppressor who cannot acknowledge their own competitive aggression, motivated manipulation, and arrogant assumption of perceptual authority — traits that, if acknowledged, would violate the MDA's moral reconstruction and the Justified-Perceiver self-concept — will show elevated projection of precisely these traits onto the outlier.

The **Projective Apparatus** is the specific operation of this mechanism in the suppressor's perception of the outlier. The suppressor's own competitive aggression — the sustained, sophisticated, proactive suppression campaign the prior corpus documents — cannot be acknowledged as competitive aggression without violating the MDA's reconstruction of that behavior as honest service. The cognitive system that maintains the suppressor's self-concept as a non-aggressor must therefore suppress awareness of the behavior's competitive character. Newman and colleagues' research predicts that this suppression activates a projection process: the competitive aggression the suppressor cannot own is attributed to the outlier.

The output of this projection is the specific form the caricature takes. The outlier is perceived as the competitive one — as calculating, as strategically positioning themselves, as working to undermine the suppressor's standing. These are precisely the behaviors the suppressor is executing. The caricature's characterization of the outlier as competitive, arrogant, and self-promoting is not merely a motivated misperception of the outlier's actual behavior. It is a projection of the suppressor's own suppressant behavioral repertoire onto the person against whom that behavior is directed.

The projection apparatus produces a specific experiential quality in the suppressor that is critical for understanding their subjective immunity to recognition of the suppression architecture: the suppressor feels like a victim. Not primarily a perpetrator — a victim. The suppressor who is projecting their competitive aggression onto the outlier perceives the outlier as the competitive aggressor and themselves as the person being subjected to that aggression. The outlier's intellectual confidence reads as an attack. The outlier's persistent engagement with a topic reads as domination. The outlier's success reads as a competitive move against the suppressor. From within the projection apparatus, the suppressor is not executing suppression. They are defending themselves against the outlier's aggression.

Fiske's (2011) analysis of social emotions directed at high-status, low-warmth others confirms the social-structural basis for this perceptual configuration. People perceived as competent but relatively cold are targets of the specific combination of acknowledgment and hostility that Fiske documents as the envy emotional profile. The suppressor who envies the outlier experiences this combination simultaneously: recognition of the outlier's competence (which cannot be fully denied) combined with hostility toward the outlier's apparent warmth deficit (which the caricature has amplified and the MAE has organized around). The hostility is felt as a response to the outlier's character, not as a response to the suppressor's own competitive threat. The emotional phenomenology of the suppression is, from the suppressor's interior, the phenomenology of justified defensive response to a genuinely difficult person.

Parrott and Smith's (1993) analysis of envy versus jealousy specifies a further dimension. Malicious envy — when the envied gap is not closeable through self-improvement — is oriented toward the reduction of the gap through harm to the envied other rather than through improvement of the self. The suppressor who envies the outlier's capacity experiences the envy as a form of motivated vigilance for the injustice of the distribution — the outlier has something the suppressor values and cannot match. This vigilance is not experienced as envy by the suppressor. It is experienced as accurate perception of an unfair social arrangement in which the outlier receives recognition they have not genuinely earned — which is precisely what the MAE's attribution machinery constructs from the ambiguous social information the suppressor encounters.

## VIII. The Developmental Consolidation Sequence

The suppressor does not arrive in the world as the consolidated operator the prior analyses document. They become that operator through a developmental sequence with identifiable stages — a trajectory from initial comparison encounter through graduated response escalation to stable, self-sustaining suppression architecture. Coie and Dodge's (1998) developmental account of aggressive behavior patterns established that children who develop stable aggressive behavioral profiles do so through a process of gradual behavioral consolidation: initial aggressive responses to threat produce social consequences that shape subsequent behavior, with each iteration either reinforcing the aggressive response (if the aggression produced social reward or reduced threat) or modifying it (if the aggression produced social cost). The suppressor's behavioral trajectory follows this model precisely, with the aggressive response being the CEA's threatened-egotism activation and the social consequences being the SRM's reward channels.

**Stage 1 — Initial Incumbent Stability.** The suppressor, prior to the outlier's developmental emergence as a visible superior, occupies a stable competitive position: the older sibling who is the smart one, the competent one, the recognized achiever in the family and peer context. The self-concept is organized around this position. The CEA is active but not acutely threatened. The suppressor's social environment confirms their standing through the authority-figure relationships they have built, the coalition they occupy, and the absence of any comparison threat sufficient to destabilize the self-concept.

**Stage 2 — Comparison Horizon Encounter.** The outlier's performance becomes visible. In a domain central to the suppressor's self-concept, the outlier demonstrates capacity that the comparison produces as threatening to the CEA. The CEA activates. The threatened-egotism profile initiates. The SEM's three available responses — reduce closeness, reduce relevance, undermine performance — are simultaneously available, and the suppressor deploys whichever is most accessible. For the older sibling whose family position makes domain exit or relationship exit structurally costly, performance undermining through the gaslighting typology and the political bypass architecture is the available path.

**Stage 3 — Graduated Response Escalation.** Initial suppressant behaviors are tested against social feedback. Those that produce coalition confirmation, authority figure validation, and the BCE's behavioral confirmation are reinforced through the SRM. Those that produce social cost are modified or abandoned. The behavioral repertoire is selected by this reward process across repeated cycles. Bandura's (1973) escalation mechanism operates: each unchallenged suppressant action reduces the inhibitory threshold for the next, and the SRM's reward channels amplify confidence in the effectiveness of the architecture. By mid-adolescence, the suppressor has a stable, socially validated behavioral repertoire that their social environment has organized itself around confirming.

**Stage 4 — Moral Architecture Consolidation.** As the suppressant behaviors become more elaborated, the MDA's operations consolidate around them: moral justifications are constructed, responsibility is displaced onto the outlier's provocations, the caricature's dehumanization deepens, and the advantageous comparison stabilizes. By the end of this stage, the suppressor has a stable moral architecture around the suppressant behavior that experiences the behavior not as suppression but as legitimate social service. The architecture is now self-reinforcing: each suppressant behavior is automatically morally reconstructed and emotionally rewarded, producing no guilt signal that could interrupt the sequence. The Justified-Perceiver Self-Concept has fully consolidated.

**Stage 5 — Operator Consolidation.** By late adolescence and early adulthood, the suppressor has consolidated into the stable operator the prior analyses document. The CEA is calibrated to the outlier. The MAE runs automatically. The MDA is well-established. The SRM provides continuous reinforcement. The NIA makes the caricature constitutive rather than merely instrumental. The operator does not need to actively decide to suppress. The architecture runs on its accumulated conditioning history, activated by the outlier's presence or by social contexts that resemble the competitive context in which the consolidation occurred.

**Stage 6 — Post-Consolidation Generativity.** The consolidated suppressor shows the generative extension Bandura's (1999) moral disengagement research predicts: the behavioral repertoire extends to new contexts and new targets who approximate the outlier's profile. The workplace transference the prior document (THE-WORKPLACE-TRANSFERENCE) specifies is the adult expression of this generativity — the cognitive schemas and behavioral repertoires consolidated against the outlier are activated in any subsequent competitive context where a comparable developmental threat is present. The suppressor no longer requires the specific outlier. They have internalized a threat-profile that triggers the consolidated architecture when any person matching that profile appears.

## IX. The Justified-Perceiver Self-Concept

The prior sections have established the motivational, cognitive, moral, identity, behavioral-conditioning, and developmental components of the suppressor's architecture. This section names the subjective result — the stable self-understanding that the architecture produces and that all six prior mechanisms jointly maintain.

The **Justified-Perceiver Self-Concept** is the suppressor's stable self-understanding as a clear-eyed, accurate, honest perceiver of the outlier's true (ordinary) capacities — the self-concept that the MAE's motivated attribution machinery constructs and that the MDA's moral architecture maintains. The JPSC is not a deliberate fiction the suppressor performs for social benefit. It is the suppressor's genuine self-understanding: they are, in their own experience, the person who sees through the outlier's performance to the ordinary capacities beneath it, who provides the honest assessment that the outlier's social environment is too intimidated or too polite to offer, who serves the outlier's long-term interests by preventing unwarranted recognition from distorting the outlier's self-concept.

The JPSC is what makes the suppressor's caricature of the outlier so durable and so devastating. It is not a strategic position that the suppressor might revise given sufficient evidence. It is an epistemic identity — the suppressor's self-understanding as someone whose perception is especially reliable precisely in the domain where the outlier's capacity generates threat. The JPSC converts the suppressor's motivated misperception into a claim to epistemic authority. The suppressor who has known the outlier since childhood, who has watched them across multiple contexts, who has received consistent confirmation from their coalition and their authority figures, believes — genuinely, without any subjective trace of distortion — that they know the outlier better than anyone else. That the JPSC is a product of motivated cognition rather than accurate observation is structurally invisible from within the JPSC itself.

Schlenker's (2003) self-presentation research establishes the social expression of the JPSC: the suppressor presents themselves to others as the most reliable source of information about the outlier precisely because their relationship history with the outlier (extensive) and their apparent concern for accuracy (genuine, within the MAE's framework) make that presentation credible. The suppressor who delivers the pre-warning is not experienced by themselves or by recipients as delivering strategic misinformation. They are experienced as a concerned person sharing difficult but necessary honesty about someone the recipient is about to encounter. The JPSC converts the suppressor into the most effective possible vector for the caricature precisely because the suppressor carrying the JPSC is not performing honesty — they are experiencing it.

## X. Predictions

**Prediction I — CEA and Esteem Contingency Dose-Response**

The Contingent Esteem Architecture predicts that the intensity of the suppressor's caricature behavior toward the outlier will correlate positively with the degree to which the suppressor's self-esteem is organized around the specific domain the outlier dominates — operationalized through Crocker, Luhtanen, Cooper, and Bouvrette's (2003) domain-specific contingencies of self-worth scale. Within-family sibling pairs should show the strongest correlation when the shared competitive domain is most parent-valorized. The threatened egotism model further predicts that this correlation will be moderated by the suppressor's prior standing in the domain: suppressors with higher prior domain standing relative to their local comparison context should show stronger suppressant responses to equivalent outlier performance, because higher prior standing represents more self-esteem investment to protect.

**Prediction II — MAE Attribution Asymmetry**

The Motivated Attribution Engine predicts a specific attribution asymmetry in the suppressor's perception of the outlier: external-unstable attributions for the outlier's successes and internal-stable attributions for the outlier's failures. This asymmetry should be significantly greater for suppressor-outlier pairs than for matched control pairs where no competitive threat history exists, and should predict suppressant behavior toward the outlier independently of the outlier's actual behavioral profile — supporting the MAE's motivated origin rather than an accuracy-of-perception account of the suppressor's behavior.

**Prediction III — MDA and Moral Self-Satisfaction**

The Moral Disengagement Architecture predicts that suppressor actors, when asked to evaluate their own behavior toward the outlier, will show significantly higher rates of the four disengagement operations (moral justification, displacement of responsibility, caricature-based dehumanization, advantageous comparison) relative to control subjects evaluating equivalent behavior toward non-outlier targets. Critically, suppressors should report significantly higher moral self-satisfaction following suppressant behaviors than control subjects engaging in similar social behaviors without the CEA's motivational architecture — distinguishing the MDA-powered suppression from ordinary competitive behavior through its positive moral-emotional signature rather than its negative guilt signature.

**Prediction IV — NIA Disconfirmation-Intensification**

The Negative Identity Anchor predicts that behavioral evidence disconfirming the caricature — an authority figure's report that the outlier showed warmth and collaborative spirit — will increase rather than decrease the suppressor's subsequent caricature maintenance behaviors, because the disconfirmation threatens the NIA's constitutive function. This distinguishes the NIA's contribution from ordinary confirmation bias: confirmation bias produces caricature maintenance in the face of neutral evidence; the NIA produces caricature maintenance intensification in the face of specifically disconfirming evidence. The intensification magnitude should correlate with the degree to which the disconfirmed caricature attributes are the mirror image of the suppressor's positive self-defining attributes.

**Prediction V — SRM Behavioral Conditioning Signature**

The Social Reward Matrix predicts that the suppressor's caricature maintenance behavior will show the classic operant conditioning signature: frequency increasing with reward schedule density and persisting past the withdrawal of individual reward events through the partial reinforcement extinction effect. Suppressors whose social networks provide the most consistent reward for caricature maintenance — most cohesive coalitions, most cultivated authority figures — should show the most elaborated and most durable suppression architectures independent of the outlier's behavioral profile. This distinguishes the SRM account from a purely motivational account: the SRM predicts suppression maintenance through external reinforcement even when the CEA's internal motivation has reduced.

**Prediction VI — Projective Apparatus Content Overlap**

The Projective Apparatus predicts that suppressors will attribute to the outlier the specific behaviors they themselves exhibit toward the outlier: competitive positioning, reputation management, strategic cultivation of authority figures, coalition formation against the other party. These attributions should significantly exceed the attributions made by neutral observers of the same outlier's behavior, and should correlate with the degree to which the suppressors' own social behavior shows the attributed qualities. The specific content of the caricature — which competitive behaviors the outlier is accused of — should overlap with the content of the suppressor's actual suppressant behavioral repertoire at rates exceeding chance.

**Prediction VII — DCS Temporal Signature**

The Developmental Consolidation Sequence predicts that the stability and elaborateness of the suppression architecture will be a function of the developmental stage at which the initial comparison threat was encountered and the density of SRM reinforcement across the consolidation period. Suppressors whose consolidation began earlier — younger age of initial encounter, longer reinforcement history — should show more automated, less context-dependent suppression behavior than suppressors whose initial encounter occurred later, when prior identity investment in non-threatened domains provided alternative self-concept anchoring that reduced the CEA's threat magnitude.
